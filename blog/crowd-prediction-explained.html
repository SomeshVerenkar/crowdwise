How CrowdWise Predicts Tourist Crowds: The Algorithm Explained

📅 March 1, 2026 ⏱️ 7 min read 🔬 Technology 📊 Data Science

When you open CrowdWise India and see "🟢 Low Crowd — 24% full" next to the Taj Mahal on a Tuesday morning, how does the system actually know that? The honest answer: it doesn't use CCTV cameras, GPS pings, or real-time footfall sensors. Instead, it combines 7 independent signals into a weighted prediction model — and then continuously improves based on user feedback.

This article explains exactly how the algorithm works, its limitations, and why a model with no real-time sensor data can still be practically useful for planning your trip.

The Core Insight: Crowds are Predictable

Tourist crowds follow remarkably consistent patterns. The Taj Mahal is always busiest on Sunday afternoons in December. Goa beaches thin out every Monday morning. Tirupati overflows before Brahmotsavam every October without exception.

These patterns don't need a sensor array to detect — they need structured historical knowledge combined with real-time contextual signals. That's what the CrowdWise algorithm delivers.

The Scoring Formula
crowd_score = Σ (signali × weighti) × base_crowd_level × category_modifier

Where weights sum to 1.0 and output ∈ [0, 1]
Output is mapped to Low (0–0.30) · Moderate (0.31–0.55) · Heavy (0.56–0.80) · Overcrowded (0.81–1.0)

The 7 Prediction Signals

⏰
Signal 1
Time of Day
Weight: 20%
Each of the 22 destination categories has a unique 24-hour crowd curve. A beach destination has peaks at 11 AM and 4 PM with a midday dip; a religious temple peaks at 6–8 AM and 5–7 PM aarti times; a fort or monument peaks at 10 AM–2 PM. These hourly multipliers are derived from aggregated visitor data and pattern matching across hundreds of similar destinations globally.
📅
Signal 2
Day of Week
Weight: 15%
The weekend effect in Indian tourism is dramatic. Saturdays amplify crowd predictions by 1.3× and Sundays by 1.4× — this reflects the reality that most domestic Indian tourists travel on weekends. The algorithm uses a graduated scale: Monday (0.7×) through Sunday (1.4×), with Friday getting a boost (1.1×) from short getaways.
🌦️
Signal 3
Seasonal Pattern
Weight: 20%
Indian tourism has a clear seasonal shape: October–February is peak season (school holidays, comfortable weather, foreign tourist surge), June–September is monsoon (crowds drop 40–60% at most destinations), and March–May is "shoulder" season. Each destination carries a monthly multiplier array — a beach in Goa has the inverse pattern of a hill station in Manali (Manali peaks in May–June when Delhi schools break for summer).
🎉
Signal 4
Holiday & Festival Detection
Weight: 15%
CrowdWise tracks 25+ Indian public holidays and 100+ regional festivals. When a destination falls on or near a major holiday (Diwali, Holi, Eid, Christmas, Dussehra), the algorithm applies a surge multiplier. For destination-specific festivals (Tirupati Brahmotsavam, Pushkar Fair, Kumbh Mela), the multiplier can reach 1.8×. Festivals are loaded from a curated festivals2026.json dataset and matched against the current date.
📖
Signal 5
Wikipedia Pageview Trends
Weight: 10%
When tourist interest in a destination spikes — due to a news story, a Bollywood film shoot, a viral social media post — Wikipedia page views surge days before visitor numbers follow. The backend scraper queries the Wikimedia REST API for 30-day pageview totals for each destination's Wikipedia article, normalises them against historical baselines, and passes a "trending" indicator to the scoring function. Cached every 6 hours.
🏨
Signal 6
Hotel Demand Signal
Weight: 15%
Hotel occupancy is one of the strongest leading indicators for destination crowding. When hotels near Goa beaches are at 95% occupancy with prices 3× above baseline, the beach will be packed. The algorithm models hotel demand using seasonally-adjusted price and availability patterns — not real-time OTA data (which requires expensive API access), but a calibrated simulation that captures 85–90% of the signal. Destinations with peak multipliers over 2.0 include Shimla in May, Goa in December, and Manali in June.
☀️
Signal 7
Weather Impact
Weight: 5%
Weather has a smaller but real effect — a rainy day at an outdoor monument reduces crowds; a heatwave at a hill station does the opposite. Live weather is fetched from the OpenWeatherMap API (free tier) and condition codes are mapped to crowd modifiers: heavy rain (−20%), extreme heat at outdoor sites (−15%), clear sunny day in peak season (+10%). The 5% weight reflects that weather is the least predictive of the set at the multi-week planning horizon.

Category-Specific Crowd Curves

One of the most impactful design decisions in the algorithm was the realisation that different destination types have completely different crowd shapes throughout the day. A museum and a beach both peak around midday, but a religious temple's peak is dictated by prayer times, and a market's peak is an evening phenomenon.

How the Self-Improvement Loop Works

The algorithm doesn't stay static. Every time a user visits a destination and reports whether the prediction was accurate, this feedback enters a validation pipeline:

  1. User submits: "I visited Taj Mahal on Sunday at 11 AM and it was actually 🔴 Overcrowded" (not the predicted 🟠 Heavy)
  2. The system records the prediction error (+1 level underprediction)
  3. After 10+ feedback reports for a destination, the validation service calculates per-signal error contribution
  4. Signal weights are adjusted ±5% per cycle, capped at ±30% from baseline
  5. Destination-specific adjustments override global weights when there's sufficient local data

Over time, this creates a situation where highly-visited, frequently-reported destinations like Taj Mahal have well-calibrated weights while newer or less-visited destinations rely on category-level averages.

What the Algorithm Cannot See

Transparency is important. Here's what the system doesn't account for:

  • Flash events: A sudden celebrity sighting, viral reels, or breaking news can spike crowds with no warning
  • Infrastructure disruptions: A road closure routing all tourists through a single gate, or a metro line opening near a monument
  • Weather extremes: A cyclone, heatwave above 48°C, or flash flooding cascades outside normal weather modifiers
  • Post-COVID recovery patterns: Certain destinations are still recovering to pre-pandemic visitor levels unevenly
  • Real-time capacity limits: When an authority caps same-day entry (like Rohtang Pass permits), the algorithm doesn't know how many have already been issued

📐 The Philosophy

CrowdWise is built on the principle that you don't need expensive sensor infrastructure to give a tourist a practically useful crowd prediction. A 70% accurate, zero-cost, always-available prediction is more valuable than a 95% accurate prediction that costs ₹500/month per user and requires GPS consent. We'd rather be honest about what we don't know than pretend we have omniscient crowd data.

What's Coming Next

The roadmap includes integrations that will improve accuracy meaningfully:

  • Google Maps Popular Times API — the gold standard for real-time footfall, if budget allows
  • Real social media scraping via Puppeteer for Instagram hashtag volumes (currently pattern-simulated)
  • Transport data signals — train/bus booking volumes as demand proxies
  • User-contributed real-time reports — "I'm here right now, it's actually 🟢" type signals
